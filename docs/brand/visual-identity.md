# Visual Identity

## Visual Character

Pool Conductor should look like a modern continuation of dependable pool and
electrical equipment: orderly, useful, and built to remain understandable for a
long time.

The current marketing site already establishes the right foundation with plain
Arial type, thin divider rules, broad white space, dark green-black ink, and one
restrained aqua accent. Extend that system instead of layering a new style on
top of it.

## Color

### Core Palette

| Role | Name | Hex | Use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary ink | Equipment Ink | `#1D2A2B` | Type, logo, buttons, diagrams |
| Accent | Pool Aqua | `#187F91` | Links, small labels, status emphasis |
| Paper | Clean White | `#FBFCFC` | Main background |
| Soft field | Water Tint | `#E7F2F2` | Quiet bands and selected states |
| Secondary field | Service Gray | `#F3F6F5` | Technical or local-control sections |
| Divider | Rule Gray | `#D5DEDE` | Borders, chart grids, separators |
| Secondary text | Muted Ink | `#586767` | Supporting copy |

### Color Behavior

- Let white and Equipment Ink do most of the work.
- Use Pool Aqua to identify action, water, or status, not as decoration.
- Keep the logo monochrome. It may appear in any single palette color when the
  surrounding application makes the contrast clear.
- Avoid blue gradients, glowing cyan, navy technology palettes, and broad fields
  of saturated color.

## Typography

### Primary Typeface

Use Arial or a metrically compatible neutral grotesk. Its familiarity supports
the brand: this is equipment meant to be used, not admired as a fashion object.

### Hierarchy

- Headlines are large, direct, and sentence case.
- Labels are small and functional.
- Body copy has comfortable line height and modest measure.
- Letter spacing remains neutral. Do not use widely tracked capitals to create
  artificial luxury.
- Numeric data uses tabular figures when available.

## Layout

- Favor clear horizontal rules and open sections over stacked cards.
- Use a stable content width and generous margins.
- Keep border radii between 0 and 5px for interface and marketing elements.
- Use alignment and spacing before adding containers.
- Let product information scan vertically in a predictable order.
- Charts should look like instruments: light grids, direct labels, and no visual
  effects that compete with the data.

## Photography

Use photography only when it proves something real:

- The controller installed in a clean weatherproof enclosure
- The equipment pad before and after an installation
- A pool professional wiring, commissioning, or explaining the system
- A homeowner using familiar controls while near the actual pool
- Clear product and terminal details

Photographs should be bright, honest, and easy to inspect. Avoid resort scenes,
anonymous luxury homes, dramatic night pools, and generic people holding phones.

## Illustration

Illustration should explain construction, wiring, compatibility, or system flow.
Use monochrome or the core palette, consistent line weights, and labeled parts.

Brand illustration may use the idea of coordinated water movement, but should
remain abstract. Do not use a conductor mascot. A literal person makes the brand
feel playful and forces every application to retell the name.

## Icons

- Use Lucide icons in the product interface.
- Use the regular stroke weight consistently.
- Pair icons with labels when the action may not be obvious to a homeowner.
- Relay icons may reflect the connected equipment, but the overall interface
  should remain quieter than the pool pad it controls.

## Motion

Motion should confirm state or reveal a relationship. Appropriate uses include
a short water-line movement during synchronization or a restrained chart update.
Avoid ambient animation, floating decoration, and looping waves.
